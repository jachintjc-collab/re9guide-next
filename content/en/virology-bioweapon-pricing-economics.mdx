<h1>Bioweapon economics — why nobody actually buys these things</h1>
<p>Every RE game implies a global bioweapon black market. RE9 references specific tier 2 G-virus pricing in BSAA recovered documents. The numbers don't add up. Here's why the canonical bioweapon market makes no economic sense.</p>

<blockquote>The RE bioweapon trade is the franchise's weakest worldbuilding. The economics fail in three places. Capcom should retire it.</blockquote>

<h2>The numbers RE9 puts on the page</h2>
<p>Per chapter 6 RPD codex entries, recovered BSAA archives list 2024 bioweapon prices: tier 2 G-virus samples at $4-12M USD per vial, Las Plagas Master strain at $20-40M, fully developed B.O.W. with handler at $80-120M.</p>
<p>For context: a Tomahawk cruise missile costs $1.4M. A modern fighter jet costs $80-200M. Capcom is pricing a single bioweapon vial in the same range as F-16s.</p>

<h2>Problem 1: deployment economics</h2>
<p>Conventional military targets are addressed with conventional weapons. The targets a bioweapon could address better than conventional weapons are extremely narrow — sealed underground facilities, hardened bunkers with biological vulnerabilities, populations you specifically want infected but not killed. None of these are common in 2026.</p>
<p>For a $12M G-virus vial to be worth buying, the buyer needs a specific scenario where conventional weapons would cost more. The intersection is essentially zero.</p>

<h2>Problem 2: handler costs</h2>
<p>RE canon implies bioweapons need specialized handling, transport, and deployment. The handler infrastructure cost dwarfs the bioweapon itself. Realistic estimate: trained handlers + secure transport + deployment vehicles + backup containment runs $20-50M for a single use case. Add it to the $12M vial cost and you're at $32-62M minimum.</p>
<p>The same money buys you a sustained conventional special operations campaign that would address most plausible mission profiles more reliably.</p>

<h2>Problem 3: blowback risk</h2>
<p>Bioweapon use risks containment failure. Containment failure means the user's own population gets infected. No rational state actor accepts that risk for low-value targets. The buyers who would accept that risk (terrorist organizations) can't afford the prices.</p>
<p>The economic floor for a buyer is "willing to risk extinction." The price ceiling is "what such buyers can pay." These don't overlap.</p>

<h2>What Capcom should do about this</h2>
<p>Retire the bioweapon market plot. Move to the "rogue research" framing instead — pathogens get loose because of accidents, not intentional sales. That's actually scary and economically credible. The "Family / Veltro buys bioweapons from market" framing has run its course.</p>
<p>RE9 already moves partly in this direction. Vivien Davies's pathogen is research escape, not sale. RE10 should commit fully.</p>

<h2>Korean community on bioweapon economics</h2>
<p>Korean RE long-form posts occasionally engage with bioweapon economics. The consensus on Naver is that the prices RE canon cites are "marketing numbers, not market clearing prices." That's the right read. Korean readers tend to treat the bioweapon market as flavor lore rather than serious worldbuilding.</p>

<h2>Verdict</h2>
<p>RE9's bioweapon economics references are the weakest piece of the game's biology lore. Read them for fun, ignore them for plausibility. The franchise's future biology arc should drop this framing.</p>
