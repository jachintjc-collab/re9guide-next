<h1>T-virus mechanism — what 28 years of canon adds up to</h1>
<p>The T-virus is RE's founding pathogen. Twenty-eight years of canon have accumulated specific claims about how it works at the molecular level. RE9 quietly reconciles inconsistencies that have bugged careful readers since 1998. Here's the consolidated picture.</p>

<blockquote>RE9's T-virus codex is the cleanest version of franchise canon we've ever had. The contradictions are mostly resolved. Took long enough.</blockquote>

<h2>The consolidated T-virus mechanism</h2>
<p>Per RE9 background terminals (consolidating canon from RE1 through RE9), the T-virus is a Progenitor-derivative single-strand RNA retrovirus with extensive cellular reorganization payload. Key mechanistic claims now consistent across canon:</p>
<p>Entry mechanism: surface receptor binding to a broadly-expressed mammalian cell surface protein (the specific receptor isn't named in canon — convenient omission). Replication cycle: classic retroviral reverse transcription + integration into host genome. Behavioral modification: through cellular reorganization of neural tissue, not through specific receptor targeting.</p>

<h2>Where canon used to contradict itself</h2>
<p>Pre-RE9 canon had three persistent contradictions: the species range (some sources said mammals only, others said all vertebrates), the dormancy capacity (some sources said immediate symptoms, others said weeks-long dormancy possible), and the transformation pathway (some sources said random mutation, others said specific phenotype targets).</p>
<p>RE9 resolves all three. Mammals only. Dormancy capacity exists but rare. Transformation pathway has specific targets but probabilistic outcomes within those targets.</p>

<h2>The real-world plausibility</h2>
<p>Real retroviruses with broad mammalian infectivity exist (foamy viruses for example). Real retroviruses don't produce the dramatic phenotype changes T-virus implies. The "cellular reorganization payload" is fictional — no real virus carries this.</p>
<p>The line where T-virus stops being credible biology and starts being narrative biology: the payload. Acceptable as fiction. Don't try to make it work as real virology.</p>

<h2>What RE9 added — the patient-zero question</h2>
<p>RE9 codex specifies that any T-virus outbreak requires a patient zero who's a deliberate or accidental contamination event. T-virus doesn't have a natural reservoir population. This means every T-virus outbreak traces to a specific lab or research breach.</p>
<p>This is good lore work. It means BSAA can prevent T-virus outbreaks by securing lab inventories. It also means historical outbreaks (Raccoon City, Spencer Mansion) trace to specific institutional failures. The biology supports the worldbuilding.</p>

<h2>The mutation question</h2>
<p>RE9 settles the long-running debate about why some T-virus exposures produce zombies, others produce Crimson Heads, others produce Lickers, others produce Hunters (when crossed with amphibian DNA), others produce Cerberus (in canids). The answer: host species + environmental conditions + time since exposure.</p>
<p>Per the codex, every T-virus infection has the same molecular mechanism. The phenotype outcomes vary because the host genetic and environmental context varies. This is the cleanest framework the franchise has ever offered.</p>

<h2>Where the canon still has holes</h2>
<p>The receptor question. Canon avoids specifying what T-virus binds to at the cell surface. This is the question that any real virologist would want answered first. Capcom keeps it vague because committing to a specific receptor would make rebuttal too easy.</p>
<p>I understand the writers' room choice. It's still the most obvious open hole in the framework.</p>
